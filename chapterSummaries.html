Chapter Summaries

  • Chapters 1-5
  • The entire rural county of Hertfordshire is buzzing with the news that a wealthy gentleman has rented a local estate.
  • Mrs. Bennet is determined to have one of her five daughters marry the single gentleman, Mr. Bingley.
  • She urges her husband to go visit with Mr. Bingley so that she may introduce him to her daughters.
  • Mr. Bennet obliges his pesky wife and goes to see the young gentleman.
  • Now Mr. Bingley can be introduced to the Bennet daughters, Jane, Elizabeth, Mary, Catherine, and Lydia. The women meet Mr. Bingley at a local ball along with his two sisters, brother-in-law, and his close friend Mr. Darcy.
  • Mr. Bingley is observed to be handsome and friendly while Mr. Darcy appears to be haughty and proud.
  • Mr. Bingley is immediately drawn to Jane Bennet, the eldest and most beautiful Bennet daughter.
  • Mr. Darcy on the other-hand is withdrawn from the crowd and refuses to dance with anyone.
  • Elizabeth overhears a conversation between Darcy and Bingley in which Darcy states that he does not find Elizabeth attractive and had no desire to dance with her.
  • Later that night Elizabeth and Jane discuss the events of the evening, Jane expresses her like for Mr. Bingley and Elizabeth expresses her extreme dislike for Mr. Darcy.
  • Jane often sees the good in people while Elizabeth focuses on their faults.
  • Elizabeth later discusses the ball again with her close friend Charlotte Lucas.
  • Charlotte points out that Darcyâ€™s pride is understandable due to his extreme wealth.
  • Elizabeth admits that her dislike for Darcy is due to the hurtful comment he made about her at the ball. His pride had wounded hers.
  • Chapter 6-9
  • As the relationship between Jane and Mr. Bingley continues to develop, the sisters Jane and Elizabeth are spending much time at his home with Mr. Bingley and his companions.
  • Bingley's sisters Miss Caroline Bingley and Mrs. Hurst are superficially kind to the Bennet sisters unlike their genuine brother.
  • When Jane receives a request to have dinner at Netherfield Park with Miss Bingley, Mrs. Bennet sends Jane to the house on horseback in the rain, so that she must stay the night.
  • Jane becomes very ill due to the exposure to the rain and is forced to remain with the Bingley's until she is well.
  • Mrs. Bennet is delighted at the idea of Jane spending a prolonged amount of time at Mr. Bingley's house.
  • Elizabeth, out of concern for her sister, walks three miles to Netherfield Park.
  • Upon her arrival Elizabeth sees that Jane is quite ill and remains at the house to help nurse her back to health.
  • While there, Elizabeth spends meals and spare time with the Bingley's and Mr. Darcy.
  • Mr. Bingley's sisters continue to be polite to Elizabeth, but go to no great lengths to conceal their disapproval of her and her behavior.
  • Mr. Darcy and Mr. Bingley however support of Elizabeth's actions.
  • The rest of the Bennet women visit Netherfield soon after and Lydia, the youngest sister, convinces Mr. Bingley to host a ball as soon as Jane is well.
  • Chapter 10-12
  • That evening, Elizabeth observes Miss Caroline Bingley excessively complimenting Mr. Darcy.
  • Miss Bingley's obvious flirtation has no effect on Mr. Darcy.
  • Darcy instead finds himself increasingly attracted to Elizabeth, but does not consider her a marriageable prospect due to her inferior family.
  • Another night in one of her many attempts to impress and interest Mr. Darcy, Miss Bingley invites Elizabeth to walk about the room.
  • During which the women converse with Mr. Darcy. Caroline is clearly jealous of Darcy's interest in Elizabeth.
  • The next day, Elizabeth and Jane return home in Mr. Bingley's carriage, after five days spent at Netherfield.
  • Darcy is relieved to have Elizabeth depart. He disliked the growing attraction he had to her, as she was clearly an unsuitable match.
  • Chapter 13-15
  • The next day, Mr. Bennet announces that Mr. Collins will be visiting that afternoon. Mr. Collins is a cousin to the Bennet's, and their closest male relative.
  • Because Mr. Bennet has all female children who cannot inherit property, Mr. Bennet's estate will go to Mr. Collins when he dies.
  • Mr. Collins is effusive in expressing his honor to be parish for the Lady Catherine de Bourgh.
  • He is pompous yet obsequious and apologetic for terms of his inheritance. Following dinner, Mr. Collins reads the girls a dull book of sermons until Lydia interrupts him to gossip about soldiers.
  • Mr. Collins later discusses his need to marry with Mrs. Bennet. After being told that Jane may soon be engaged, Mr. Collins shifts his intentions to Elizabeth.
  • The next day, Mr. Collins joins the sisters on a trip to the nearest town. In town, the girls are introduced to a young soldier, Mr. Wickham.
  • Mr. Wickham charms the sisters, particularly Elizabeth.
  • While with Mr. Wickham, the girls encounter Mr. Darcy and Mr. Bingley, Darcy and Wickham are noticeably hostile towards one another.
  • The girls convince their aunt to invite Mr. Wickham to a gathering the next night.
  • Chapter 16-17
  • Wickham impresses all in attendance at the party.
  • There, Wickham tells Elizabeth of his connection to Mr. Darcy.
  • They had grown up together, Wickham's father working for Darcy's, and in his will, Darcy's father had left Wickham a sum of money.
  • Wickham tells Elizabeth that Darcy had refused to give him the money promised him, and because of it he was forced to join the militia instead of the church as he wanted.
  • Elizabeth believes everything Wickham says, reinforcing her contempt for Darcy.
  • Jane warns Elizabeth to hear both sides of the story before she passes judgment on Mr. Darcy.
  • Elizabeth hopes to see Mr. Wickham again at Mr. Bingley's ball.
  • Chapter 18-23
  • Mr. Wickham does not attend the ball, to Elizabeth's disappointment.
  • Her night is made even worse when she must dance with the two men whom she most dislikes, Mr. Collins and Mr. Darcy.
  • Mrs. Bennet loudly discusses her hopes for the marriage of Jane and Bingley, her sister Mary performs an awful song, and Mr. Collins gives a ridiculous speech.
  • Elizabeth is extremely embarrassed by the behavior of her family at the ball.
  • Mr. Collins proposes to Elizabeth the next day.
  • Elizabeth politely declines, but he assumes that her refusal is an attempt to increase his desire for her and persists.
  • Elizabeth offers Mr. Collins a more forceful rejection, which Mrs. Bennet finds appalling.
  • Mr. Bennet defends his favorite daughter's decision and Mrs. Bennet is defeated.
  • A few days later, Jane receives a letter from Caroline Bingley informing her that she, her brother, and the rest of their party will be departing for London and will not return.
  • Elizabeth's close friend Charlotte Lucas announces that she is engaged to Mr. Collins, for lack of any better prospects.
  • Elizabeth is upset to hear that her friend is leaving.
  • Mrs. Bennet is distraught that both Elizabeth and Jane no longer have suitors or marriage in the foreseeable future.
  • Chapter 24-26
  • Miss Bingley sends Jane another letter implying that Mr. Bingley will marry Mr. Darcy's sister and reconfirming that they will not return to Netherfield.
  • Mrs. Bennet's brother and his wife come to visit the Bennet's.
  • The Gardiners invite Jane to return to London with them, hoping it might lift her spirits. Jane accepts.
  • Mrs. Gardiner notices the growing attachment of Elizabeth and Mr. Wickham.
  • While she likes Wickham, Mrs. Gardiner knows that his lack of money prevents him from being a proper match for Elizabeth.
  • She warns Elizabeth of the dangers of becoming too involved with a man like Wickham and the embarrassment it could cause her family.
  • Jane leaves for London with the Gardiners and corresponds with her family through letters.
  • Mrs. Gardiner also writes Elizabeth inquiring about Mr. Wickham.
  • Elizabeth informs her aunt that Wickham is now interested in another girl, who had recently come into money.
  • Elizabeth finds that she is indifferent about Wickham pursuing another, leading her to conclude that she never loved him.
  • Chapter 27-34
  • Elizabeth travels to Rosings to visit her friend Charlotte in March.
  • On the way she stays with the Gardiners for a night and accepts their invitation to go on a tour after her visit with Charlotte.
  • The day after her arrival at the home of Charlotte and Mr. Collins, Elizabeth is introduced to Lady Catherine de Bourgh and her invalid daughter.
  • Lady Catherine is a harsh and rigid woman whom after interrogating Elizabeth concludes that the Bennet daughters were poorly raised.
  • Some days later Mr. Darcy and his cousin arrive at the manor to visit their aunt, Lady Catherine.
  • That night Elizabeth discusses Darcy with his cousin, Colonel Fitzwilliam, while playing the pianoforte at Lady Catherine's insistence.
  • Elizabeth sees Darcy and Fitzwilliam many times when staying with Charlotte.
  • On one of the instances Fitzwilliam mentions that Darcy recently rescued a friend from entering into a marriage with an inferior woman.
  • Elizabeth assumes that the relationship in question is Bingley and Jane's. This infuriates Elizabeth.
  • Right after Elizabeth discovers the information about Jane, she is approached by Mr. Darcy.
  • Darcy declares that he is desperately in love with Elizabeth despite the inferiority of her birth and proposes marriage.
  • Elizabeth is unable to control her rage and confronts Darcy about what he did to Mr. Wickham and his sabotage of Jane and Bingley's relationship.
  • She states that she could never marry a man as proud as Mr. Darcy.
  • Chapter 35-42
  • The next day, Mr. Darcy gives Elizabeth a letter.
  • In it he admits fault in preventing the Mr. Bingley from proposing to Jane, but admits that he did not realize the attachment that Jane had developed to his friend.
  • Darcy then explains what had actually happened with Mr. Wickham. Darcy had given him the money that was left to him, which Wickham promptly spent.
  • In an attempt to acquire her wealth, Wickham proposed to Darcy's young sister, Georgiana.
  • When he discovered that he would not receive Georgiana's money, Wickham left her.
  • Elizabeth begins to realize that her all of her ill opinions of Mr. Darcy were not based in truth.
  • Elizabeth leaves Rosings and meets up with Jane in London; the sisters then make their way home.
  • Elizabeth tells Jane the truth about Mr. Wickham, but they decide not to tell anyone else about him.
  • Mrs. Bennet foolishly agrees to allow Lydia to spend the summer with the wife of a colonel in Brighton with all of the soldiers.
  • In July, Elizabeth joins her aunt and uncle on a tour of the countryside.
  • The Gardiners suggest a tour of Pemberly, Mr. Darcy's home, after they assure her that Mr. Darcy will not be there, Elizabeth agrees to go.
  • Chapter 43-45
  • The beauty of Darcy's estate impresses Elizabeth.
  • She listens to his housekeeper give a very positive description of him to her aunt and uncle.
  • Elizabeth imagines what her life would have been like if she had accepted Darcy's proposal.
  • As they are walking the estate, Darcy appears. He joins the party on their walk and behaves very politely.
  • Elizabeth confesses to him that she only came because she thought he would not be there.
  • Darcy tells her that he has just arrived to prepare for guests, including Mr. Bingley.
  • He offers to introduce her to his sister, Georgiana. Darcy, Georgiana and Bingley visit Elizabeth and the Gardiners the next day and invite them to have dinner at Pemberly.
  • Mr. Bingley's sisters are present at the dinner.
  • They are very critical of Elizabeth and her family. Afterward, Miss Bingley tries to disparage Elizabeth to Darcy.
  • He admits to her that he considers Elizabeth one of the most beautiful women that he knows.
  • Chapter 46-49
  • There are two letters waiting for Elizabeth when she arrives back at the inn.
  • They are form Jane informing her that Lydia has run away with Mr. Wickham and it is likely that they have not married.
  • A scandal such as this would destroy the entire family's reputations.
  • Distraught, Elizabeth runs into Mr. Darcy and tells him the horrible news.
  • Both Elizabeth and Darcy blame themselves for not exposing Wickham earlier. Elizabeth and the Gardiners immediately return home.
  • On the way back Elizabeth tells her aunt and uncle of Wickham's shameful past.
  • When they arrive, they discover that Mr. Bennet has gone to London to find the couple; Mr. Gardiner goes to London as well.
  • The two men are unsuccessful and Mr. Bennet returns home to Longburn. Mr. Gardiner remains in London and soon informs the family that he has located Lydia and Wickham and they will agree to marry for a small income.
  • Knowing the kind of man that Wickham is, the family assumes that Mr. Gardiner has paid Wickham a sizeable amount of money to get him to agree to the wedding.
  • Despite the disgraceful circumstances, Mrs. Bennet is overjoyed to have a daughter married.
  • Chapter 50-55
  • Elizabeth's feelings for Darcy continue to grow.
  • She realizes how unlikely it is that he would propose a second time because of her initial refusal and Lydia's scandalous marriage to Mr. Wickham.
  • Mr. Bennet reluctantly allows Lydia and Wickham visit, before they move north.
  • The visit is a difficult one, as Wickham and Lydia appear unaware of the trouble that they have caused their family.
  • During the brief visit Lydia mentions that Mr. Darcy was at her wedding.
  • Shocked, Elizabeth wrote to her aunt for more information.
  • Mrs. Gardiner's response states that Mr. Darcy is the one who located Wickham and Lydia and paid Wickham the large sum he required for the marriage.
  • News arrives that Mr. Bingley will return to Netherfield Park.
  • Bingley and Darcy visit the Bennet home, once they arrive in Longburn.
  • Mrs. Bennet is excessively rude to Mr. Darcy; ignorant of the exceptional favor he had done for the family by helping Lydia.
  • Bingley returns to the Bennet's a few times before he proposes to Jane, she gladly accepts.
  • Chapter 56-61
  • Lady Catherine de Bourgh suddenly shows up at the Bennet house, a week after Jane's engagement.
  • Lady Catherine demands to speak with Elizabeth and tells her that she has heard rumors that Elizabeth is engaged to Mr. Darcy. Lady Catherine is appalled at the idea, given her nephew's superior station in life and his betrothal to her daughter.
  • Elizabeth informs lady Catherine that she and Darcy are not engaged. Lady Catherine, infuriated by Elizabeth's words, departs
  • Mr. Darcy soon visits Bingley at Netherfield, and visits the Bennets with Bingley. They all go on a walk, but Darcy and Elizabeth walk behind the group.
  • Elizabeth thanks Darcy for his assistance with Lydia. He assures her that he only did for Elizabeth and that he still loves and wants to marry her.
  • Elizabeth tells him that her feelings have changed and she would love to marry Darcy.
  • That night, Elizabeth confesses her love of Darcy to Jane and the next day Darcy asks Mr. Bennet for permission to marry Elizabeth.
  • Mr. Bennet is doubtful of Elizabeth's love for Darcy, but his daughter assures him by telling her father of all of the things Darcy had done for her and their family.
  • After Jane and Elizabeth are married, Jane and Bingley move into an estate near Pemberly and the sisters see each other often.
